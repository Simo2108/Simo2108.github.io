History

The first Italian Grand Prix, in addition to be the first great international sporting manifestation after the war, had a considerable technical importance.
The internal combustion engine, during the war had in fact taken steps by giant and the European automotive industry (especially the French and Italian) boasted a good production.
The race was an opportunity to compare the engine development of various countries; more than between drivers and cars a challenge between nations, therefore.
The Montichiari circuit, near Brescia, was chosen for the race. It consisted of two straights (5 and 6 kilometers long) which were joined to the base by a stretch of road - built from scratch - of 1400 meters and at the top by a sharp curve, called "parabolic".
Of the 20 entered only 6 crews showed up for the race. Three Fiat cars, led by Wagner, Sivocci and Bordino e three Ballot cars driven by Di Palma, Chassagne and Goux. The Fiat cars, of very recent production (they had not participated participated in the French Grand Prix because workers' strikes had delayed their construction) faced the first demanding race in Brescia.

The Fiat drivers enjoyed the favor of forecasts, both because they were Italian and because the their engine developed 110 horsepower against the 107 of the opponents, allowing them higher speed on the straight.
The Ballot, built in 1920 and already tested in Indianapolis and Le Mans, made up for the lower power with a more extensive experience. Also, on the Montichiari circuit (to be covered 30 times), many judged the decisive "parabolic" curve, and the Ballot, equipped of brake booster, had the possibility of "to detach" after the others and therefore to run the curve faster.
There was also among the drivers of the two manufacturers almost equal values. Bordino, ex- mechanic of Vincenzo Lancia, possessed a boldness and a vigorous driving, equal, if not superior, to that of Goux. On both, the critics of the time said, the handicap of youth weighed heavily (Bordino was already 33 years old, but then, for a pilot, they were few).
Wagner and De Palma were the two very expert foremen, while Sivocci and Chassagne were called "clocks" for their regularity.

At the checks all the cars result weighing around 900 kilos, therefore no only problem for the challenge between the three reds Fiat and the three blue Ballot.
The mechanics in the race (who, due to the regulations, had to run with the drivers) were: Lebouc (Goux), Laly (Chassagne), De Paolo (De Palma), Lampiano (Wagner), Morganti (Sivocci) and Bruno (Bordino).
The race started with an amazing Bordino's "exploit": he took the first lap ride in 7'20", against Wagner's 7'27" and Goux's 7'37". Even in later laps the speed of Bordino kept increasing, followed by Wagner, Chassagne, Goux, De Palma and Sivocci. After 4 laps Wagner stopped for replacing a pneumatic , and Chassagne was overtaken by Goux, which moved into second position. Bordino, in the lead, fell below 7 first minutes, with an average speed of over 150 km/h(new world record), earning an average of ten seconds per lap on the French.
It seemed like a triumphal march, but it did not last long: on lap 13 Bordino was forced to pit for the replacement of a tire, with Goux passed ahead. The beautiful comeback of the Fiat's driver lasted only two laps: the breakup of the pump of the lubricant forced it upon withdrawal. The race, amidst the scorching disappointment of the Italian fans, was over. Goux won undisturbed with and average speed of over 144 km/h. The bitterness was great. The defeat was justified by the lack of experience of the drivers and Italian's cars.
This depended, it was said, on the fact that in Italy there was no track on which to train and test the cars (reason that favored, the following year, the birth of Monza racetrack). And the little driving experience of the Italians was proven, according to the papers of the time, from the tires: the Pirelli's tires had allowed the French to finish the race without stopping, while Wagner, for example, had to replace seven tires. It is also necessary to take present the different braking system and also the different driving technique: the French, more refined, faced the curves avoiding damaging skidding and violent brake strokes, the Italians, instead, relied on impetuousness.
